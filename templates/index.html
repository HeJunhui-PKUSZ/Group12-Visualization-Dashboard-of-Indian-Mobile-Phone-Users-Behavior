           To understand users' consumption habits, we selected two consumption-related features: the amount users spend monthly on e-commerce platforms and the amount users recharge or pay for mobile phone plans monthly for clustering. The effectiveness of clustering largely depends on the number of clusters, so it is necessary to determine an appropriate K value before performing K-Means clustering. After standardizing the data, we used the elbow method and silhouette coefficient to assist in selecting the optimal number of clusters. First, we presented the trend of clustering performance indicators as the number of clusters varied. From the graph, it can be observed that when K=4, the slope of the error reduction curve tends to flatten, and the silhouette coefficient reaches its peak, indicating a relatively optimal clustering effect. Therefore, the final number of clusters determined is 4. Based on the analysis of the two-dimensional scatter plot of the clustering results, the clusters show clear boundaries while the data points are relatively compact, which indicates a good clustering performance.
           We output the cluster centers of user consumption behavior. According to the results, through K-Means clustering, users are divided into four distinct consumption groups, with significant differences in the center values of e-commerce spending and monthly recharge fees across the clusters. Users in Cluster 0 exhibit high levels of both e-commerce spending and monthly mobile plan expenses, representing a typical high-consumption group that pursues higher digital service quality. Users in Cluster 1 are characterized by high e-commerce spending but low monthly recharge fees, indicating that this group emphasizes online shopping while reasonably controlling plan expenses. Users in Cluster 2 have low spending in both e-commerce and mobile plans, showing limited demand for e-commerce and plan services, making them a low-consumption user group. Users in Cluster 3 have relatively low monthly e-commerce spending but high mobile plan expenses, indicating that they are not frequent online shoppers but rely heavily on mobile plans, representing a high-frequency social user group.

           From the word cloud comparison, it is evident that there are significant differences in the primary uses of mobile phones between high-consumption and ordinary-consumption users. In the word cloud for high-consumption users, terms such as "Entertainment" and "Work" occupy more prominent positions, while "Gaming" and "Education" also carry notable weight. This indicates that such users not only invest more in high-frequency usage scenarios like entertainment and gaming but also rely on their phones for multifunctional purposes such as work and learning. Overall, their usage patterns demonstrate higher intensity and a more diversified range of functional demands.In contrast, the word cloud for ordinary-consumption users highlights terms like "Gaming" and "Entertainment", while the weight of "Social Media" and "Work" appears relatively scattered. This suggests that their mobile phone usage leans more toward daily entertainment and basic social needs, with a weaker correlation between consumption behavior and usage scenarios.
           Overall, the word cloud results indicate a certain correlation between users' consumption levels and the structure of their mobile phone usage purposes. High-consumption users tend to exhibit more frequent, comprehensive, and value-oriented usage patterns.
